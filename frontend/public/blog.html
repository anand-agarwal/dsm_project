01 — MotivationWhy Does This Matter?

Child marriage — any formal or informal union where at least one party is under 18 — remains a deeply entrenched crisis in South Asia. India alone is home to roughly a third of the world's child brides. Despite the Prohibition of Child Marriage Act (2006), enforcement gaps persist. The practice continues to cluster around poverty, educational exclusion, and social marginalisation in ways that are uneven and often invisible in aggregate national statistics.

This project asks two questions:

Primary: What socio-economic, educational, religious, and demographic factors are the strongest predictors of child marriage rates across Indian states?

Comparative: How did the relative importance of these factors shift between the 2001 and 2011 censuses — and do driver patterns differ across Scheduled Caste, Scheduled Tribe, and religious sub-populations?

"An evidence-based, state-level view of these factors can inform policy interventions, prioritise resource allocation, and help NGOs identify the most vulnerable pockets of the population."

By running separate LASSO models for each census year and each population sub-group, we move beyond a static snapshot. We ask not just what predicts child marriage, but what changed over a decade — and why.

02 — DataThe Census of India

Our primary source is the Census of India, administered by the Office of the Registrar General & Census Commissioner. We draw on two waves: the 2001 census as a historical baseline, and the 2011 census as the main analytical dataset, which provides individual-year age granularity and richer sub-group breakdowns.

Which tables did we use?

The census is not a single clean table — it's a sprawling archive of sub-tables, each covering one intersection of population characteristic (marital status, education level, economic activity) × demographic group (general, SC, ST, religion) × geography (district / state). We integrate 15+ sub-tables:

File structure and parsing challenges

Each census table is distributed as one .xls file per state/UT. The 2001 files use the legacy binary BIFF8 format (requiring xlrd), while 2011 files use the XML-based format (requiring openpyxl). Our pipeline auto-detects the engine from each file's magic bytes.

Within each file, the first 7 rows form a multi-row merged-cell header block — data begins at row 8, where each row encodes one geographic unit × area type × age group combination.

State names also required careful canonicalisation across two census waves that use different spellings and historical names ("Orissa" → "Odisha", "Uttaranchal" → "Uttarakhand", "Pondicherry" → "Puducherry" and so on).

03 — Index ConstructionThe CMPR

The central metric we construct is the Currently Married Proportion Rate (CMPR) — the share of individuals in a given population segment, sex, and age bracket who are currently married.

CMPRs,g,b = ( Currently married in segment s, sex g, bracket b ) / ( Reference population for s, g ) × 100

We compute CMPR across four canonical age brackets, chosen to align with policy definitions of child marriage:

Bracket LabelAge RangePolicy Significance
age_below10< 10 yearsExtreme early marriage
age_10_1310–13 yearsSevere child marriage
age_14_1714–17 yearsChild marriage — below legal age
age_18_2118–21 yearsYoung adult marriage; transition zone
⚠ Approximation Note C-02 reports age in 5-year bands, so age_10_13 maps to the "10–14" band (includes one extra year). C-08/C-12 provide individual-year rows for ages 7–19, so age_18_21 captures only ages 18 and 19 from those tables.

Four DataFrames, four population slices

We construct separate DataFrames for each population sub-group, each in long format (140 rows = 35 states/UTs × 4 age brackets):

  • df_total — general population (from C-02, C-06, C-07)
  • df_SC — Scheduled Castes (from C-02 SC, C-08 SC, C-12 SC)
  • df_ST — Scheduled Tribes (from C-02 ST, C-08 ST, C-12 ST)
  • df_religion — six religious communities: Hindu, Muslim, Christian, Sikh, Buddhist, Jain (from C-03 Appendix, C-09)

Together these produce 130+ distinct metric columns covering literacy rates, below-primary education shares, school attendance rates, child labour indicators, and the CMPR itself at multiple demographic intersections.

05 — ModellingLASSO Regression

Why LASSO?

With 15 candidate predictors and only ~35 state-level observations, ordinary least squares would be severely overfit. We use LASSO (Least Absolute Shrinkage and Selection Operator), which applies an L1 penalty that automatically zeroes out weak or redundant predictors. The non-zero coefficients directly answer our question: which variables are the strongest drivers?

LASSO is also well-suited here because census education variables are highly correlated (literacy rate and illiteracy rate are definitional complements). LASSO's L1 penalty shrinks one of a correlated pair toward zero, producing stable and interpretable solutions. The resulting coefficients are directly comparable in magnitude across variables — a coefficient of −4.3 on female SC literacy means a one-standard-deviation increase in that variable is associated with a 4.3-unit decrease in CMPR_SC_female.

⚠ Critical Modelling Choice df_total contains conditional CMPR rates (e.g., CMPR_illiterate_female). These are not independent predictors — they share the same denominator as the target, creating a tautological model if used as inputs. We use them only for education-gradient analysis. Only structural rates (literacy rate, dropout rate, child labour rate) are used as regression predictors in SC/ST/religion models.

Model specification and cross-validation

The regularisation hyperparameter α is selected via Leave-One-Out Cross-Validation (LOO-CV), which maximises training data at each fold — appropriate for n < 40. All predictors are standardised to zero mean and unit variance, so coefficients are on a comparable scale.

CMPR[d,g,b,y] = β₀ + Σⱼ βⱼxⱼ    subject to    Σⱼ |βⱼ| ≤ 1/α

We fit one model per (group × gender × bracket × year) combination. The same specification is fit independently on 2001 and 2011 data, enabling year-on-year comparison of which predictors enter the model and at what coefficient magnitude.

Python · cmpr_lasso_analysis.py (key implementation)
from sklearn.linear_model import Lasso
from sklearn.model_selection import LeaveOneOut, cross_val_score
import numpy as np

# Alpha grid: 100 values log-spaced between 1e-3 and 1e2
alphas = np.logspace(-3, 2, 100)

best_alpha, best_cv_r2 = None, -np.inf
for alpha in alphas:
    model = Lasso(alpha=alpha, max_iter=20000)
    loo = LeaveOneOut()
    scores = cross_val_score(model, X_std, y, cv=loo, scoring="r2")
    cv_r2 = np.nanmean(scores)  # nanmean handles rare negative folds
    if cv_r2 > best_cv_r2:
        best_cv_r2 = cv_r2
        best_alpha = alpha

# Warn if alpha hit the ceiling (uninformative model)
if best_alpha == alphas[-1]:
    print(f"⚠ Alpha ceiling hit for {group}-{year}: model uninformative")

# Fit final model on full data
final_model = Lasso(alpha=best_alpha, max_iter=20000).fit(X_std, y)
coefs = dict(zip(feature_names, final_model.coef_))

06 — ResultsKey Findings

LASSO Finding 01

Female literacy: the single most universal driver

Appears in every model across all groups and both census years. Literacy (Total F): β = −4.90 in 2001. Literacy (SC F): β = −4.31. Its coefficient is consistently negative — higher literacy predicts lower child marriage, for both female and male CMPR [1][4].

LASSO Finding 02

SC: attendance → male dropout shift by 2011

In 2001, SC girls who work and attend school had lower marriage rates (β = −3.20). By 2011, that protection had gone — replaced by male child labour dropout (β = +1.21) as the dominant risk signal [1].

LASSO Finding 03

ST models: best fit; below-primary entrenched

ST models achieve R²cv = 0.711 — the highest in the analysis. CL attending (ST F) had β = −6.55 in 2001, the largest coefficient in the entire study. Below-primary share of ST females persists with β ≈ +1.7 in both years [3][4].

LASSO Finding 04

Religion models: group-specific single predictors

By 2011, each religion reduces to one predictor: female literacy for Hindus; below-primary share for Muslims (β = +2.71, the strongest 2011 religion predictor); sign-reversing below-primary for Christians [4].

LASSO Finding 05

Universal coefficient attenuation 2001 → 2011

Every persistent predictor weakened between the two waves — except Below primary (ST F), which barely moved (+1.72 → +1.66). The Total female model's CV R² fell from 0.238 to −0.059 [2][5].

Female literacy predicts male child marriage too

One of the more striking results: female literacy is nearly as strong a predictor of male child marriage rates as it is of female rates. This suggests it is not operating purely through individual women's agency but proxies a broader community-level educational environment — a culture of schooling that delays marriage for all members [1][4].

Male child labour dropout predicts female child marriage

The Pearson correlation between male child labour dropout (SC) and female CMPR SC at age 14–17 is r = 0.55 — nearly as high as the female illiteracy correlation. This points to a joint household distress mechanism: when boys drop out of school to work, girls in the same household are married off. Policy interventions targeting only girls' education may miss this entirely [1].

"When boys drop out to work, girls are married off — a joint distress signal that cross-gender education statistics alone cannot reveal."

07 — Temporal AnalysisWhat Changed, 2001 → 2011?

The comparative analysis design fits identical model specifications on 2001 and 2011 data separately, then classifies each predictor as: persistent (non-zero in both years), emerged (zero in 2001, non-zero in 2011), or dropped (non-zero in 2001, zeroed in 2011).

Macro context: India 2001–2011

The decade between the two censuses was one of significant — but uneven — transformation. National literacy rose from 64.8% to 74.0%, with female literacy increasing from 53.7% to 65.5%, and the male–female literacy gap narrowing from 21.6 to 16.7 percentage points [3]. GDP growth averaged 6–8% per year, but gains were unevenly distributed, with persistent rural and lower-caste deprivation shaping household decisions around schooling and child marriage [2][5]. Programmes like the Sarva Shiksha Abhiyan reduced spatial gradients in school attendance, compressing between-state variation in literacy — which directly explains the weakened predictive power of literacy in 2011 models.

Universal coefficient attenuation

With very few exceptions, persistent predictors show weaker coefficients in 2011 than in 2001. This is a system-wide pattern pointing to two simultaneous processes: genuine progress as average CMPR fell across states, and structural change that made single-factor explanations less adequate. The Total female model is the extreme case — CV R² collapsed from 0.238 in 2001 to −0.059 in 2011, literally worse than predicting the mean.

The SC/ST attendance-to-dropout shift

Both SC and ST models show a parallel structural change: in 2001, school attendance while working (CL attending) was a strong negative predictor — girls who worked but still attended school had meaningfully lower marriage rates. By 2011, this protective effect had vanished entirely, replaced by school dropout as the dominant risk signal. This parallel across two independent sub-group models strengthens the interpretation considerably.

Religion models: group-specific predictors, declining power

All religion-based models show substantially reduced CV R² between 2001 and 2011, and by 2011 most reduce to a single predictor. Each religion has a distinct dominant predictor: for Hindus it is female literacy (β = −2.19 in 2011); for Muslims it is below-primary share (β = +2.71) — the strongest single predictor in any 2011 religion model, indicating that partial education is insufficient protection and the relevant threshold is middle school or above [1][4].

The Christian model presents the most striking anomaly: Below primary (Christian F) was a strong negative predictor in 2001 (β = −4.39), meaning states with more below-primary educated Christian women had lower marriage rates — the opposite direction to every other group. By 2011 it flipped to positive (β = +1.41). This sign reversal likely reflects a composition change: in 2001, Christian-majority areas were concentrated in the south and northeast (historically low-CMPR regions), whereas by 2011 Christian communities had expanded geographically, reversing the correlation [3][4].

"The mechanisms driving child marriage are not static. Any policy framework built only on a 2001-era understanding of the problem would miss the household economic distress signal that has since emerged."

08 — ClusteringGeographic Patterns: State Groupings

To complement the regression analysis, we apply Ward-linkage hierarchical clustering on LASSO-selected predictor features for each population group and census year. All models use k = 3 clusters, confirmed by elbow curves. The analysis groups states by CMPR profile at the age_14_17 bracket, revealing which states are genuinely similar in their child marriage landscape — and how those groupings shifted over the decade.

SC Female clustering

In 2001, three clusters emerged with well-separated mean CMPRs. The high-burden Cluster 1 (mean CMPR = 36.9) was dominated by Bihar, Jharkhand, Rajasthan, Uttar Pradesh, and West Bengal — the north + east bloc — with mean CMPR nearly three times that of the low cluster. Cluster 2 (mean 12.4) was composed almost entirely of southern states and small Union Territories, consistent with the LASSO finding that high female literacy in these states suppresses marriage rates.

The most important shift: Uttar Pradesh exited the highest-burden cluster between 2001 and 2011 — a notable improvement — moving to the middle cluster alongside Odisha and Himachal Pradesh. Bihar, Jharkhand, and Rajasthan remained in Cluster 1 throughout, confirming their status as structurally resistant high-burden states.

ST Female clustering

ST clustering reveals different geographic patterns. In 2001, the northeast states (Mizoram, Manipur, Nagaland, Arunachal Pradesh) and Lakshadweep cluster firmly in the low-CMPR group (mean 11.7), reflecting higher female literacy and different social norms. The high-burden cluster (mean 30.0) is composed entirely of central and eastern mainland states — where the below-primary share identified by LASSO is highest.

By 2011, the ST cluster structure shifted less favourably than SC: the middle tier converged upward, with more states joining the medium-to-high cluster. This is consistent with the LASSO finding that the ST below-primary predictor barely attenuated — structural educational exclusion persisted, and dropout replaced attendance as the operative risk mechanism.

Religion-stratified clustering

Hindu: In 2001, Cluster 1 (mean CMPR 27.3) was anchored by Bihar, Rajasthan, Madhya Pradesh, Jharkhand, UP, and Chhattisgarh — the same high literacy-deficit states identified by LASSO. By 2011, Bihar separated into its own extreme outlier cluster (mean 22.9, only 3 states) driven by Bihar's extraordinarily high Hindu female CMPR.

Muslim: The persistence of Below primary (Muslim F) as the sole LASSO predictor in 2011 (β = +2.71) is reinforced by the clustering: the high-CMPR Muslim cluster is precisely the set of states where Muslim female educational attainment is truncated at below-primary level. States like Mizoram and Nagaland appearing in the high Muslim CMPR group reflects small Muslim population sizes there, making CMPR volatile.

Christian: Lakshadweep isolated into its own cluster by 2011 with mean CMPR ≈ 0.0 — effectively no Christian child marriage reported. The sign reversal of Below primary (Christian F) between 2001 and 2011 discussed in Section 7 is visible in how the cluster composition shifts across the decade.

Cross-group clustering: the most revealing visualisation

The most powerful analysis clusters all 35 states simultaneously on all six female CMPR dimensions (SC F, ST F, Hindu F, Muslim F, Christian F, Total F) for 2011. The result is striking:

10 — LimitationsCaveats

Honest data science requires surfacing its own blind spots. The key limitations of this analysis are:

  1. Age band mismatch. C-02's 5-year bands mean age_10_13 includes age 14, and age_14_17 excludes it. Some bracket labels are approximations.
  2. Partial age_18_21 coverage. In C-08/09/12, ages 20–21 are embedded in the "20–24" band and cannot be extracted, so this bracket covers only ages 18–19 from those sources.
  3. C-12 age floor. The school attendance dataset begins at age 5; the age_below10 bracket covers only ages 5–9, not 0–4.
  4. Small sample size. With 35 observations per bracket, all models are in a small-n regime. LOO-CV mitigates overfitting, but CV R² values — especially for religion models — should be treated as hypothesis-generating, not confirmatory. The SC male 2011 model (11 predictors, CV R² = 0.166) shows signs of overfitting; its predictor list should not be taken as reliable causal drivers.
  5. State-level aggregation. District-level variation, which may be substantial, is not captured. State averages can mask high-prevalence pockets.
  6. Cross-sectional data. The 2001–2011 comparison approximates a longitudinal design but does not constitute panel estimation; unobserved time-invariant state characteristics are not controlled for.
  7. Underreporting. Child marriage is illegal. Some incidence is almost certainly underreported in census responses; our CMPR values are lower bounds.
  8. CMPR as proxy. "Currently married" captures individuals married at any age who are in that marital state — not necessarily those who married as children.
  9. Religion predictors limited to education. Occupation, income, and urbanisation — likely important for religion-based differences — are not available at the religion × state level in the census sub-tables used. This is the most likely explanation for the sharp CV R² declines in religion models between 2001 and 2011.
  10. Hindu male 2001 model. LASSO selected zero predictors (α at upper bound of 100, CV R² = −0.06). This reflects near-zero explainable variance using available predictors, not a model error. Results for this group-year should not be interpreted.
  11. Christian Below primary sign reversal. The sign reversal (−4.39 in 2001 → +1.41 in 2011) is interpreted as a composition effect but cannot be confirmed without district-level or longitudinal data.

11 — ConclusionWhat We Found

This project constructs a comprehensive, multi-dimensional dataset of child marriage rates and their correlates across all Indian states, drawing on 15+ census sub-tables from the 2001 and 2011 waves. By computing CMPR stratified by sex, scheduled status, and religious community, we produce a state-level analytical resource that enables both descriptive comparison and formal regression modelling — and makes it all accessible through an interactive dashboard.

The LASSO analysis and hierarchical clustering together yield eight substantive findings:

  1. Female literacy is the universal protective driver. It appears as a selected predictor in every model across all groups and both census years — the most cross-cutting result of the entire analysis. Its coefficient is consistently negative (Literacy Total F: β = −4.90 in 2001; β = −0.99 in 2011), with no exception across groups or target genders.
  2. SC models: attendance-to-dropout shift between 2001 and 2011. School attendance even under child labour (CL attending SC F: β = −3.20) was the second-strongest predictor in 2001. By 2011 it vanished entirely, replaced by male child labour dropout (β = +1.21) — a joint household distress pathway, not a girl-specific educational one.
  3. ST models: best predictive fit; below-primary share entrenched. ST female 2001 achieved the highest CV R² in the study (0.711). CL attending (ST F) at β = −6.55 was the largest coefficient in the entire analysis. Below-primary (ST F) remained virtually unchanged at +1.72 → +1.66 — structurally entrenched and not resolving over the decade.
  4. Religion models: declining explanatory power; group-specific dominant predictors. By 2011, each religion reduces to a single predictor: female literacy for Hindus (β = −2.19); below-primary share for Muslims (β = +2.71, the strongest 2011 religion result); an anomalous sign-reversing below-primary for Christians, reflecting geographic composition change.
  5. Universal coefficient attenuation 2001 → 2011. Every persistent predictor weakened substantially — reflecting both genuine progress and structural change that made single-factor models less adequate. The Total female model's CV R² collapsed from 0.238 to −0.059.
  6. SC clustering: UP improved; Bihar–Jharkhand–Rajasthan did not. Uttar Pradesh exited the highest-burden cluster across the decade. Bihar, Jharkhand, and Rajasthan remained in the top cluster throughout both waves.
  7. ST clustering: middle tier converged upward. Unlike SC, more ST states moved into the medium-to-high burden cluster by 2011 — consistent with persistent educational exclusion at the primary level.
  8. Cross-group clustering confirms state-level structural burden. Bihar, Jharkhand, MP, Rajasthan, and West Bengal are simultaneously high-burden across SC, ST, Hindu, Muslim, and Christian CMPR. The dominant explanation is state-level governance and infrastructure failure, not community-specific practices. Conversely, Goa, Kerala, and Manipur are low across every dimension.
"Protection is not community-specific — it is environmental. The same states that protect SC girls also protect ST girls, Muslim girls, and Hindu girls."

The analysis is complemented by an interactive dashboard at project-bachpan.vercel.app that visualises CMPR patterns across states, brackets, and sub-groups for non-technical audiences, and exposes the LASSO coefficient comparisons and clustering results as an explorable interface.
